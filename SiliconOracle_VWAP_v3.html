VWAP & oracle methodology · v3 · internal working draft

A continuous compute oracle a perp can actually liquidate against.

v3 separates the oracle into a fast Mark Price for intraday marks, funding and liquidations and a slower Index Price (Completed-Rental VWAP) for integrity and settlement, anchored once a day to the TradFi print. The benchmark earns trust by publishing components first, bounding worst-case manipulation, and never going dark on the liquidation path.

01 · The gap (verified)

The TradFi compute benchmarks physically cannot mark a perp.

Bullish launched CMWTI-USDC-PERP on 6 May 2026 because CME closes ~49 hours every weekend and the Strait of Hormuz shock — the IEA's largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market — proved institutions must hedge through the dark window. Compute has the same gap, worse: CME + Silicon Data is explicitly the "first daily GPU benchmark," and ICE + Ornn (OCPI), while marketed as a live index, settles its futures Asian-style on the average of daily values. Both are cash-settled reference rates over completed (lagging) prints, on a TradFi cadence. Neither is a continuous, high-frequency feed a perp DEX can mark and liquidate against minute-by-minute.

The opening: a compute perpetual needs a continuous 24/7 price for funding and liquidations. We build the continuous engine; the TradFi index becomes a once-a-day settlement anchor. But "continuous" is an oracle-engineering claim, not a VWAP claim — see §03.

03 · The core fix

A completed-rental VWAP cannot tick fast enough to liquidate. Split the oracle.

Where the prior draft is wrong: it promises a "tick-level 24/7 feed" but specifies the engine as a Completed-Rental VWAP. A completed rental only prints when a multi-hour lease finishes and telemetry confirms uptime. The feed's update frequency is therefore bounded by lease completions, not wall-clock ticks, and every verified print is stale by roughly the lease duration. You cannot mark and liquidate a perp minute-by-minute off an input that arrives every few hours and looks backward. The one high-frequency signal you have — the order book — is exactly the thing the draft demotes to "never traded volume."

This is how every serious perp already resolves it: a fast Mark Price drives liquidations and funding, and a slower Index Price keeps the mark honest. Silicon Oracle should adopt the same split.

Tier A · fast

Mark Price

Liquidations, intraday marks, funding basis. Built from a multi-venue order-book microprice (depth-weighted bid/ask mid across DePIN + partnered books), bounded to the Index by a clamp band. Updates every few seconds. Never null.

drives liquidations
Tier B · slow

Index Price (DLV)

Completed-Rental VWAP over a defined rolling window. The truth tether: the Mark may not stray more than a set % from it. Updates on lease completion. May carry-forward / null in dead windows.

tethers the mark
Tier C · daily

Settlement Anchor

TradFi daily print (OCPI / Silicon Data). A once-a-day reasonableness check the Index is reconciled against; divergence beyond a band raises a governance flag.

daily reconciliation
Mark / Index tether (liquidation-safe)
mark_microprice_t =
  Σ_v depth_weight_v · microprice_v,t     # across venues v

index_dlv_t = completed_rental_vwap (rolling window, §05)

# tether: the mark cannot run away from verified truth
clamp_band = f(liquidity_score, dispersion)      # e.g. ±5%..±15%
mark_price_t =
  clamp(mark_microprice_t,
        index_dlv_t · (1 - clamp_band),
        index_dlv_t · (1 + clamp_band))

# liquidation path NEVER returns null:
if index_dlv_t is stale/null:
    widen clamp_band, raise confidence_interval,
    keep publishing mark with degraded-confidence flag

05 · The VWAP, with a window

Define the accumulation window, or the VWAP is undefined.

Completed-Rental VWAP — windowed & time-decayed
for each admitted completed rental i in window W:

price_i  = total_paid_usd_i / verified_gpu_hours_i
volume_i = verified_gpu_hours_i

# recency weighting so a 23h-old lease does not
# count the same as one that just settled:
decay_i  = exp(-(t_now - t_i) / tau)        # tau ~ 6-12h

dlv_index =
  Σ(price_i · volume_i · decay_i) / Σ(volume_i · decay_i)

# two published windows:
#   dlv_settlement : trailing 24h, no decay  -> daily/settlement
#   dlv_live       : trailing 4-6h, decayed  -> Index tether (§03)

robustness: also publish weighted-MEDIAN rental price.
VWAP (a mean) is whale-sensitive in thin markets;
the median is the manipulation-resistant cross-check.
Reconcile the two staleness mechanisms. The prior draft had both a "trailing 24h window" and a "4h carryforward" with no stated relationship. They are not the same control: the window defines what's in the VWAP; carryforward defines what happens when the window empties. State both, and which series each applies to.
06 · Verified hours & price basis

Admit by rule; disclose that the price is per usable hour.

Verified GPU-hours admission rule
usable_hours_i =
  observed_rental_duration_i × uptime_ratio_i

verification_tier_i =
  max_proof_level_satisfied_by_record_i

admission_rule:
  if verification_tier_i >= minimum_required_tier:   # threshold, by rule
      verified_gpu_hours_i = gpu_count_i × usable_hours_i
  else:
      exclude rental and publish exclusion_reason

no hidden multiplier:
  verification changes admission status, not volume weight
Basis disclosure (comparability bug). Because verified hours are uptime-discounted, price_i is a price per usable hour, which reads higher than the contracted price the buyer actually paid. Two consequences: (1) the DePIN Discount Spread understates the true discount — you inflated the DePIN numerator; (2) your DLV will read systematically higher than Ornn / Silicon Data on identical rentals if they quote contracted price. Publish both a contracted-price VWAP and the uptime-adjusted one, and state the basis loudly.
07 · Oracle-mode spec

The four things a perp DEX actually diligences.

"Would a venue settle on this?" is not an IOSCO question — it is four concrete questions. The prior draft specifies the ingredients but none of these. Each must be a published number.

1 · Worst-case manipulation bound

Manipulation cost = cost to move the cheapest meaningfully-weighted input, not the average. Deep Web2 ballast does not help if a 30%-weighted on-chain leg is 100× cheaper to push. Publish: "$X moves the index Y%" and require X > backed open interest.

2 · Source-outage re-weighting

A blend lowers null risk, but dynamic re-weighting is itself an attack: knock out a source to shift weight onto the manipulable leg. Define how weight reallocates on outage and cap how far it can shift in one interval.

3 · Lag budget

Manipulation-resistance (slow Web2, time-decay) and freshness trade off directly; lag is toxic-flow fuel. State a target: e.g. Mark ≤ 30s, Index ≤ window. The clamp band (§03) is how you buy resistance without lagging the Mark.

4 · Trust model

A weighted, filtered, governed, partly-off-chain composite is a trusted oracle (CF Benchmarks / Chainlink category), not a trustless primitive. Adoption runs on reputation, SLAs, clean source rights, and this published manipulation analysis — say so; don't imply cryptographic guarantees.

Worst-weighted-input manipulation bound
for each input source s with weight w_s:
  cost_to_move_s(δ) = capital to move source s price by δ
                       (order-book depth, lease notional, etc.)

index_move(δ) ≈ Σ_s w_s · δ_s          # bounded by caps/filters

# the bound that matters:
manip_cost(Δ) =
  min over attack vectors of
  { capital required to move index by Δ }

publish: manip_cost(5%)  and  manip_cost(10%)
gate:    require manip_cost(Δ_liq) > open_interest_backed
08 · Source map & data rights

Two filters per source: rentals-vs-offers, and rights.

Each source is judged on (1) does it expose completed rentals or only offers, and (2) data rights — scraped / unclear-rights data is barred from commercial publication. That is the second reason (besides depth) to license.

10 · Integrity controls

Why a venue can settle on this — and where the wash vector hides.

ControlRule
Binary verification gateAdmit a rental only at the minimum proof tier (telemetry / attestation). Admits or rejects — never secretly scales volume.
Rental-type stratificationPrice spot / on-demand / bulk / reserved separately so a discounted enterprise batch cannot masquerade as the spot clearing price.
Concentration capsNo provider, buyer, or buyer-provider pair exceeds a set share of the rolling window. (See §08 Hyperbolic tension.)
Wash-lease defensesCounterparty-distinctness scoring, entity-graph / address clustering, minimum lease duration, price-band screen.
Carryforward limitsIf no valid rentals print, carry prior value max 4h, flag after 60 min, null after expiry. Applies to the Index only — never the Mark (§03).
Settlement-ready statusPublished flag gated on min GPU-hours, provider diversity, freshness, exclusion rate, and data rights.
The wash vector the draft misses: volume-wash at market price. Existing defenses catch below-market prints. An attacker controlling both sides can wash at the market price to manufacture volume — clearing settlement-ready thresholds and dominating concentration while staying inside the price band. Counterparty-distinctness helps; name this vector explicitly and gate volume-based readiness on distinct-counterparty volume, not raw volume.
Don't let the price-band screen self-reference. Screening rentals against the offer index re-imports the spoofable supply signal you isolated; screening against a historical band can erase genuine demand spikes/crashes as "outliers." Screen on counterparty structure and verification, not primarily on price proximity to a band.
11 · Composite policy

Experimental until the components have history.

Policy: the product is the component series. Show the composite only as an experimental field. Do not use it for settlement, liquidations, or NAV until backtested. And do not use a multiplicative weight chain (base × liquidity × quality × rights) — that is the same "one low factor collapses to zero" failure you (correctly) reject for the offer index. Use additive, bounded weights.

14 · Bottom line

The wedge is real. The fix is splitting the oracle.

What's right: the continuous-engine-vs-daily-anchor framing, the Bullish-WTI analogy (verified), and the honest source/rights map are strong and more rigorous than either TradFi competitor's public methodology.

Ship a two-tier oracle: a fast, never-null Mark Price (multi-venue order-book microprice) for liquidations and funding, tethered to a slower Completed-Rental VWAP Index for truth, reconciled daily to the TradFi anchor. Publish the manipulation bound, the lag budget, the source-outage rule, and the trust model as numbers — that is the gap between a dashboard and a feed a perp DEX will sign off on.
